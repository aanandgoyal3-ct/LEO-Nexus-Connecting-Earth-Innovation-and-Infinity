Key Technologies — Simple & Practical

Below are technologies you can describe on the website and explain how they work in simple words.

Modular architecture

Ship separate modules (power, lab, docking). Modules plug together like building blocks. If one part breaks, it can be swapped instead of throwing away the whole station.

Why it helps: cheaper long-term, easier upgrades.

In-orbit servicing & refuel

Robots or small ships dock with a module to repair, refuel, or replace parts. This increases lifetime and reduces waste.

Practical step: design standard docking ports for any servicing vehicle.

Electric propulsion (efficient)

Ion or Hall thrusters use little fuel but push for a long time — good for moving modules or changing orbit slowly. New fuels like iodine are compact for small vehicles.

Deorbit & debris mitigation

Systems like drag sails or small thrusters lower a dead satellite's orbit so it burns in the atmosphere. Active removal craft (concepts exist at NASA) can target big broken objects.

This follows NASA’s deorbit guidance (e.g., 25-year aim). :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

Autonomy & AI for operations

Onboard software checks health of systems and acts fast if a problem appears. This lowers ground cost and reaction time.

Power: solar + storage

Solar panels produce electricity; batteries store it. Good energy management shares power between modules and keeps instruments running at night.

How these connect (simple flow)

  1. Launch modules to LEO → modules dock to form a hub.
  2. Power module supplies electricity → labs & payloads run experiments.
  3. Servicing ships dock to refuel/repair → increases lifetime.
  4. When a part is retired, deorbit module safely removes it or attaches a drag sail.
